# Example — Business / pricing problem (worked, condensed)

**Problem:** "If I raise my consulting price I lose price-sensitive clients, but at
the current price I'm overworked and under-earning."

## 1. Framing
System: the consulting offer + pricing. Super: market, referrals, competitors.
Stakeholders: you (income, sustainable load), clients (value, affordability).
Main useful function: *offer delivers outcome → client pays.* Harmful:
*overwork* (excessive load), *under-pricing* (insufficient capture). Constraint:
limited hours, existing client base, reputation. Goal: higher earnings + lower
load without gutting the client base.

## 2. Function analysis (business)
| Tool | Action | Object | U/H | N/I/E |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Hourly delivery | produces | client outcome | U | Normal |
| Flat-low price | captures | value | U | Insufficient |
| Manual everything | consumes | your hours | H | Excessive |
Trimming candidate: bespoke delivery for low-tier clients.

## 3. Root cause
Under-earning + overwork → all clients get the same high-touch service at a low
flat price → **root cause: no segmentation of value or price; time sold instead
of outcomes** (controllable). Leverage: separate clients by value and decouple
income from hours.

## 4. Contradiction
- **Engineering:** IF price ↑ THEN income ↑ BUT price-sensitive clients leave.
- **Physical:** the price must be **high** (capture value / cut load) and **low** (retain price-sensitive clients).
- Break via separation by **condition** (segment) and **parts/whole** (tiering).

## 5. Resources
Existing: reputation, current clients, past deliverables (reusable templates),
testimonials, referral network, idle "method" knowledge. Underused: your IP could
be productized (course/template) — income without hours.

## 6. IFR
You earn more while working less, and each client pays **in proportion to the
value they get** — low-value clients are served cheaply and almost automatically.

## 7. Methods: Business TRIZ + Physical Contradiction (separation) + Resource + Ideality + Trimming.

## 8. Solutions (tagged)
- Conservative: raise price for new clients only [Separation in time]; grandfather existing [IP-9].
- Creative-realistic: **tiered offer** — premium high-touch (high price) + lite self-serve (low price) [Separation by condition / IP-1 Segmentation]; productize the method as templates [IP-26 Copying].
- Non-obvious TRIZ: low-tier clients **serve themselves** via your template + async review [Trimming Rule B / IP-25]; refer price-sensitive clients to a junior/partner and take a cut [Trimming Rule C / IP-24 Intermediary].
- High-risk/high-upside: drop hourly entirely, move to outcome/value-based pricing [IP-13 Other way round].
- Minimal test: introduce a single productized "lite" tier alongside current offer.

## 9. Evaluation (excerpt)
| Solution | Impact | Feas | Cost | Speed | Risk | Rev | Cplx | Ideal | Total |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Tiered (premium + lite) | 5 | 4 | 4 | 3 | 3 | 4 | 3 | 5 | 31 |
| Price up, new clients only | 3 | 5 | 5 | 5 | 4 | 5 | 5 | 3 | 35 |

## 10. Experiment plan
- **Best candidate:** add a self-serve "lite" tier (keeps price-sensitive clients, frees your hours) + raise new-client premium price.
- **Experiment:** for 6 weeks, quote new leads the two-tier menu; track take-rate.
- **Data:** lead→client conversion, average revenue per client, hours per client, churn of existing clients.
- **Success:** avg revenue/client +20% AND hours/client −20% with no existing-client churn.
- **If works:** productize a second tier and raise premium again. **If fails:** narrow to new-client price increase only (the safe fallback).
